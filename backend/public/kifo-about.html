Our story

Kifo was built for the moment words don't come easily — when grief is fresh, when memory is all you have left. We believe every person's story deserves a place that honors it with care, permanence, and beauty.

Why we exist

Memorial pages shouldn't feel like funeral brochures.

When James Williams lost his mother Margaret in November 2024, he searched for a place to create something worthy of her — a page that felt as warm and alive as she was. What he found were outdated platforms plastered with ads, or clinical obituary templates that felt cold and impersonal.

Kifo was built in response to that gap. We believe a memorial should feel like sitting in someone's favourite room — full of their photographs, their music, the stories that made them who they were.

We exist to give every family the tools to create something truly beautiful, with AI that helps when words don't come easily, and a platform that respects the sanctity of the space.

What we believe

The principles we build by

♾️
Permanent by default
A memorial should outlast any subscription. Every page created on Kifo stays online forever — on the free plan, the Premium plan, all of them. Memory doesn't expire.
🚫
No ads. Ever.
A memorial page is a sacred space. We will never place advertisements on them, never sell user data, and never let advertising influence how we build. Our business is sustained by subscriptions alone.
🌿
Warmth over efficiency
We obsess over every detail of how grief meets design. Typefaces, spacing, tone — every choice is made to feel human, warm, and worthy of the people being remembered.
🔒
Privacy as standard
Families control exactly who sees a memorial. Fully public, private, or anywhere in between — with granular guest permissions and no dark patterns nudging toward visibility.
✦
AI in service of memory
Our AI tools exist to help people find words when grief makes them hard. Never to automate or cheapen — always to assist, to suggest, and to hand back control to the humans who matter.
🤝
Built for families
Memorials are collaborative. We make it easy for dozens of people — across generations, time zones, languages — to contribute to a single shared space of remembrance.
The story behind Kifo

Built from grief. Built with care.

The idea for Kifo began at a kitchen table in Chicago, in the weeks after Margaret Rose Williams passed away. Her son James — a developer — found himself frustrated by every existing memorial platform he tried. They felt clinical, ad-covered, and completely unworthy of the woman he was trying to honour.

"I wanted something that felt like her favourite room — warm, full of photos, with her music playing softly. Everything out there felt like an obituary column. I decided to build what I wished existed."
— James Williams, Founder

Kifo launched in early 2024 with a single memorial page — Margaret's. Within a month, friends and family of other families had created dozens more. Word spread quietly, through grief support groups, funeral directors, and families who found Kifo at the hardest moments of their lives.

Today Kifo serves families in over 120 countries. We've never taken venture capital. We've never run ads. We grow because families share us with other families — and because we believe deeply that the product itself should be the only thing that earns trust.

The name Kifo comes from the Swahili word for death — chosen not to be morbid, but because we believe death deserves to be spoken plainly, and that the life lived before it deserves to be remembered beautifully.
